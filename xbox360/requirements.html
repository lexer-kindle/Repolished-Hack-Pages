What you'll need

What You Will Need

Gather and verify everything below before you start. Rushing into a 360 project with the wrong hardware is the most common way to brick a console or waste money.

  • The target console, with its motherboard revision and current dashboard/CB version already identified. This dictates every other choice you make.
  • A NAND programmer to read and write the console's flash memory. Popular options include the xFlasher 360, the older JR-Programmer, or a Raspberry Pi Pico running PicoFlasher. The Pico route is inexpensive and widely available, making it a great budget choice.
  • J-Runner with Extras, the community software that ties the whole process together. It reads your NAND dump, extracts the all-important CPU key, builds the modified image, and writes it back.
  • Your console's CPU key, which J-Runner can recover from a clean NAND dump. This key is unique to each console and is required to build a working image. Back it up somewhere safe.
  • A glitch chip for RGH 1.2 and RGH 2 installs (common boards include the Matrix Glitcher, Ace V3, or CoolRunner-style chips). RGH 3 needs no glitch chip and uses only a couple of wires.
  • A Post Fix Adapter if you are working on a Corona V3 or newer slim board.
  • Soldering equipment: a temperature-controlled iron, fine-gauge wire (such as 30 AWG kynar), flux, and good lighting. Steady hands and patience matter more than expensive gear.
  • A reliable power supply and a known-good SD/USB storage device for the dashboard and homebrew, plus a multimeter for checking points and continuity.

Finally, set aside a clean, static-safe workspace and budget enough uninterrupted time. Read the methods and installation pages fully before buying parts, since the exact glitch chip and wiring depend on your board.
