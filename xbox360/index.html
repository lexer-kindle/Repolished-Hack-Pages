Xbox 360 Homebrew Guide

Welcome to a community-style walkthrough for unlocking the homebrew potential of the original Xbox 360 family of consoles. This guide is written for hobbyists who want to run legal homebrew, preserve their own legally owned game backups, build a media center, or revisit the console through emulation. It is not intended to help anyone pirate software or cheat online, and following these steps will permanently ban your console from Xbox Live.

The single most important thing to understand before you begin is that there is no one-size-fits-all method for the Xbox 360. Unlike many other consoles, the correct approach depends entirely on two things: your console's motherboard revision and its CB (bootloader) and dashboard version. A method that works perfectly on one board can be impossible on another. Because of this, the very first task in any 360 project is identification, not soldering or flashing.

Broadly, the available techniques fall into a few buckets. JTAG is a pure software-and-NAND method that boots instantly, but it only works on older "phat" consoles that were never updated past a vulnerable dashboard. The various Reset Glitch Hack approaches (RGH 1.2, RGH 2, and RGH 3) use a brief, carefully timed reset signal to slip past the security checks, and they cover the vast majority of consoles, including the slim models. R-JTAG is a hybrid used to bring JTAG back to phats that were patched.

This guide is organized into focused pages: a compatibility breakdown, a requirements checklist, an explanation of each method, a high-level installation overview, a homebrew software tour, troubleshooting, an FAQ, and credits. Read the compatibility and methods pages before buying any hardware. Take your time, work methodically, and identify your hardware first.
