Nintendo DS Hacks Guide

Run homebrew and your game backups from a single SD-card flashcard that drops straight into the game slot.

What this is for

The original DS and DS Lite are modded almost entirely through slot-1 flashcards - cartridges with a microSD slot that load DS ROMs and homebrew. There's no console exploit or firmware install; you simply insert the flashcard. Optional hardware mods include replacement shells, buttons, and aftermarket IPS screens. Both consoles also have a slot-2 (GBA) port.

What to check first: Modding uses slot-1 flashcards, so no console firmware update or flashing is required - just insert the card. Pick a flashcard verified to work on your model and check vendor compatibility lists, since many R4-style cards are clones of varying quality. The DS Lite is brighter with more parts support; the original DS has better GBA slot-2 ergonomics.

⚠️ Before you start: Console modding carries real risk — an interrupted install or the wrong method/file for your exact model can leave hardware unbootable. Identify your model first, back up anything important, and proceed at your own risk.

What you'll need

  • An original DS or DS Lite console
  • A reputable slot-1 flashcard (R4i-SDHC, Ace3DS+, or DSTwo)
  • A microSD/microSDHC card formatted FAT32
  • Flashcard kernel/firmware files and your legally-obtained DS ROMs
  • A computer with a microSD reader
  • For hardware mods: tri-wing/Phillips drivers and (for screens) soldering tools

How it works — the process

This walkthrough names the tools at each stage so you know what each one is and what it's for. Always use the latest release of each named tool.

  1. Choose a flashcard
    Select a known-good slot-1 card (R4i-SDHC, Ace3DS+, or a DSTwo for added emulation) from a trusted vendor.
  2. Prepare the microSD
    Format the card FAT32, then copy the card's kernel/firmware files and your DS ROMs and homebrew.
  3. Boot and play
    Insert the flashcard into slot-1 and power on; the card's menu loads from SD with no console changes.
  4. Add homebrew (optional)
    Load DS homebrew and emulators onto the SD card; a DSTwo additionally runs GBA/SNES via its onboard CPU.
  5. Optional hardware mods
    Swap in a replacement shell, buttons, or an IPS screen kit, opening the console and soldering where required.

Which option should I pick?

For most people an R4i-SDHC or Ace3DS+ is the cheapest, simplest choice. Choose a SuperCard DSTwo for built-in GBA/SNES emulation; pick the DS Lite over the original DS for a brighter screen and better parts availability.
