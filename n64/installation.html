Nintendo 64

Installation

Disclaimer: Hardware modding can permanently damage your console if done carelessly. Soldering work in particular risks lifting pads, bridging traces, or harming delicate ribbon connectors. Proceed at your own risk, work slowly, and if you are not confident with a soldering iron, consider the no-solder options or a professional installer. Always back up cartridge saves before experimenting.

Installation Process

Because the three modding goals are independent, your installation path is whatever subset you choose. Work through only the phases that apply to your plan, in this order.

  1. Choose your goal. Decide whether you want SD loading, import support, better video, or some combination. Each phase below is optional and self-contained, so there is no need to do all of them.
  2. Set up a flashcart. Format your SD or microSD card, typically to FAT32, and copy the menu firmware for your cart along with your ROM files. Insert the card into the EverDrive-64 X7, X5, or SummerCart64, then power on. The menu should appear and let you select a title. No console modification is required, and the cart works in any region of console.
  3. Handle imports if needed. For an NTSC-U to NTSC-J swap, trim or remove the two plastic tabs inside the console's cartridge slot so foreign shells fit, or trim the tabs on the cartridge itself. For anything involving PAL, fit a passthrough adapter such as the N64 Passport between the cartridge and console, or perform a donor-PIF chip swap if you want a permanent internal fix.
  4. Install a video mod if desired. For HDMI, open the console, seat the UltraHDMI or PixelFX board, and make the ribbon and signal connections per the kit's instructions. For native RGB, confirm your board revision first, then install the matching amplifier mod, soldering carefully to the correct points. Reassemble only after you have verified your connections.
  5. Test everything. Power on and confirm each modification independently. Check that the flashcart menu loads and games run, that an imported cartridge boots, and that your new video output displays a clean, stable picture. If something fails, isolate which phase introduced the problem before reassembling fully.

Tackle one phase at a time and test after each. Combining mods is supported, but verifying them separately makes troubleshooting far easier should anything go wrong.
