Nintendo NES

FAQ

Frequently Asked Questions

Is the blinking light a sign my NES is dead?

Almost never. On a front-loader the blinking power light is the classic symptom of a worn 72-pin connector failing to read the cartridge, which trips the lockout chip into a reset loop. Refurbishing or replacing the connector fixes it the vast majority of the time.

Should I refurbish or replace the 72-pin connector?

Both work. Refurbishing keeps the original Nintendo connector by cleaning and gently re-tensioning its pins, and many enthusiasts prefer it because aftermarket replacement quality varies. Replacement is faster and avoids delicate bending, but buy a well-reviewed connector so it grips cartridges correctly without damaging them.

Which EverDrive should I buy?

The EverDrive N8 Pro is the standard choice. Get the 72-pin version for a North American NES or the 60-pin version for a Famicom. It offers an in-game menu, save states, cheats, and wide mapper support.

Can I play Famicom games on my NES flashcart?

Yes, with a 72-to-60-pin adapter to bridge the different cartridge pinouts. To also hear Famicom expansion audio you need the EXP audio mod and the N8 Pro's EXP9-SND jumper set.

Do I need the RGB mod?

Only if you want the best possible picture. The stock console outputs composite or RF video; the NESRGB mod adds a clean RGB signal ideal for modern displays and upscalers. It requires soldering and is permanent, so it is optional.

Will the top-loader blink like the front-loader?

Generally no. The top-loader uses a more durable connector and rarely shows the blinking-light fault, though it outputs RF-only video from the factory.

Is modding hard?

The connector fix is beginner-friendly with basic tools. The RGB and EXP audio mods require soldering skill and patience, so practice first if you are new to it.
