PlayStation 1

Methods

Methods Explained

There is no single "best" way to mod a PS1 — the right choice depends on your board revision, your soldering confidence, your budget, and whether you want to keep using physical discs. Here are the real options.

PSNee (open-source modchip)

PSNee is the modern community standard. It is free, open-source firmware that runs on a cheap microcontroller and injects the region/anti-piracy signal the console expects, letting it boot CD-R backups. It supports essentially every board revision from PU-7 to PM-41. Pros: inexpensive, well-documented, broad compatibility, keeps the original drive. Cons: requires soldering several points to the motherboard, and PAL out-of-region use needs extra wiring to BIOS pins. Best for hobbyists comfortable with a soldering iron who want a low-cost, disc-based solution.

MM3 / OneChip / Mayumi

These are older commercial modchips from the console's heyday. They accomplish the same job as PSNee but are now harder to source, and their pinouts vary by board. Pros: proven hardware if you already have one. Cons: dated, scarce, no ongoing updates. Mostly relevant to people restoring a machine that was chipped years ago.

xStation (optical drive emulator)

xStation is an ODE that replaces the laser assembly entirely. Instead of burning discs, you load .bin/.cue images from an SD card through an on-screen menu. Pros: no disc wear, no laser to fail, no CD-R burning, sidesteps region locking, near-silent. Cons: costs more than a chip, kit must match your model, and you give up playing original discs unless you keep the drive. Ideal for people whose laser is dying or who want a clean digital library.

Terraonion MODE

The MODE is a premium multi-console ODE that also supports PS1. It offers a polished interface and a small display. Pros: high build quality, multi-system appeal, strong feature set. Cons: the most expensive option here. Best for enthusiasts who want a flagship ODE, perhaps across several consoles.

The swap trick (no hardware)

The swap trick exploits the boot sequence of the very earliest fat consoles — SCPH-1000, early 1001, and 3000 — by swapping discs at a precise moment so the console authenticates an original but loads a backup. Pros: no soldering, no parts. Cons: only works on those earliest units, the timing is finicky, and the repeated motion accelerates wear on an already old drive. Treat it as a curiosity or a stopgap, not a permanent setup.
