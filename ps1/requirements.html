What You'll Need

Your shopping list depends on which route you take. The modchip path involves soldering; the ODE path involves replacing the drive and preparing an SD card. Below is what each route generally calls for.

Common to every route:

  • A PS1 console whose board revision you have already identified (PU-7, PU-8, PU-18, PU-22, PU-23, or PM-41).
  • The correct screwdrivers to open the case — typically a Phillips set, plus a security bit for some shells.
  • A clean, static-safe work surface and good lighting.
  • The original games you intend to back up, and a way to dump them to disc images.

For the modchip route (PSNee, MM3, OneChip, Mayumi):

  • The modchip itself — for PSNee, an Arduino-style microcontroller (such as an ATtiny or Arduino board) flashed with the latest PSNee firmware.
  • A temperature-controlled soldering iron with a fine tip, thin solder, flux, and thin wire for the board connections.
  • Helping hands or a vise, plus solder wick and isopropyl alcohol for cleanup.
  • A revision-specific wiring diagram so you connect to the correct points (and BIOS pins on PAL).
  • CD-R media to burn your backups, and burning software that supports PS1 image formats.

For the ODE route (xStation, Terraonion MODE):

  • The ODE kit matching your exact console model and board.
  • A quality SD card formatted as the device requires.
  • Your games as .bin/.cue (or supported) image files copied to the card.
  • Minimal soldering for some installs, though many ODE kits use ribbon connectors in place of the original drive.

Always download the latest tool and firmware releases before starting, since fixes and broader compatibility land regularly. Sourcing genuine parts from reputable sellers reduces the risk of dead-on-arrival hardware.
