Installation Process

Read this first. Modding a PS1 means opening the console and, for chip routes, soldering directly to a fragile vintage motherboard. Mistakes can permanently brick your console, damage the drive, or injure you with a hot iron. This page describes the process at a conceptual level only — it contains no exploit code and is not a substitute for a revision-specific wiring diagram. Always use the latest tool and firmware releases, double-check every connection against documentation for your exact board, and proceed entirely at your own risk. If you are not confident soldering, consider an ODE kit or a qualified installer.

At a high level, every install follows the same arc: figure out what you have, pick a method that fits, install the hardware, prepare your media, and verify before reassembling.

  1. Identify your hardware
    Open the case and read the board revision silk-screened on the motherboard (PU-7, PU-8, PU-18, PU-22, PU-23, or PM-41). Note your model number and whether the console is NTSC or PAL, since PAL adds a BIOS-level region check.
  2. Choose your method
    Match the revision and your goals to a method: PSNee for a low-cost solder-in chip, xStation or Terraonion MODE for a no-laser ODE, or — only on the earliest fats — the swap trick. Gather the matching kit, tools, and the correct wiring diagram.
  3. Prepare the workspace
    Set up a static-safe, well-lit area. For a chip, ready a temperature-controlled iron, flux, thin wire, and cleanup supplies. For an ODE, lay out the kit and confirm it matches your model before touching anything.
  4. Install the hardware
    For a modchip, solder each wire to the documented point for your revision, keeping leads short and tidy; on PAL, add the BIOS pin connections for out-of-region support. For an ODE, remove or disconnect the original drive and fit the emulator per its instructions, often via ribbon connectors with minimal soldering.
  5. Prepare your media
    Chip route: burn your owned-game backups to quality CD-R using software that supports PS1 image formats. ODE route: copy your .bin/.cue images to a properly formatted SD card.
  6. Test before closing up
    With the board still accessible, power on and confirm a backup or image boots cleanly, including an out-of-region title if relevant. Inspect for shorts and recheck solder joints if anything fails.
  7. Reassemble
    Once everything boots reliably, route wires so nothing is pinched, reseat the shielding, and close the case. Do a final boot test fully assembled.
