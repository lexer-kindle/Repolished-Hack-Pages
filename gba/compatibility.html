Which Game Boy Advance Do You Have?

Before buying any parts, identify your exact model. The three common variants look similar but differ hugely in screen quality, and that difference decides whether a screen mod is worth doing. Flip your console over and read the model number molded into the plastic: it begins with AGB on the original brick-style GBA and AGS on the clamshell SP.

Model Form factor Stock screen Needs a screen mod?
AGB-001 Original horizontal GBA Reflective, no light; dim and hard to read Strongly recommended. Use an IPS or backlight kit, ideally one with a V2 ribbon.
AGS-001 Clamshell SP Front-lit; brighter than AGB-001 but washed out and grainy Recommended. IPS/backlight kits give a dramatic upgrade.
AGS-101 Clamshell SP Factory backlit; bright, vivid, high contrast No. It already looks great. Leave it alone.

The takeaway is simple. If you own an AGS-101, you have the best stock display Nintendo ever shipped on this platform, and your only project is a flashcart. If you own an AGB-001 or AGS-001, a modern screen kit is the single biggest quality-of-life improvement you can make.

One practical wrinkle: the original AGB-001 is prone to vertical tearing artifacts with some screen kits unless you use a panel that ships with a V2 ribbon cable designed to avoid the problem, so pick your kit accordingly. SP installations have a different challenge: modern IPS panels are slightly larger than the original glass, so the front shell almost always needs trimming to fit. Neither issue is a dealbreaker, but knowing about them up front saves frustration.

If you do not have an AGS-101 but want that backlit look without an aftermarket kit, transplanting a genuine AGS-101 panel into an AGS-001 shell is a proven option covered later in this guide.
