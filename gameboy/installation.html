Game Boy

Installation

Disclaimer: Modding opens your console and involves soldering on a vintage board. You risk scratching the shell, cracking a ribbon cable, or damaging the mainboard, and you will void any notion of original condition. Work slowly, double-check that every part matches your exact model (DMG, MGB, or CGB), and proceed at your own risk. If you have never soldered, practice on scrap first.

Installation Walkthrough

The order below builds from easiest to hardest, so you can stop wherever your comfort runs out and still have a working console.

  1. Identify your model. Confirm whether you have a DMG, Pocket (MGB), or Color (CGB) and lay out only the parts cut for that console. This single check prevents the most common build-ruining mistake.
  2. Set up the flashcart. No disassembly needed. Format the microSD card, copy your menu files and ROMs you legally own, insert it into the EverDrive-GB X7 or EZ-Flash Junior, and boot the console to confirm the menu loads and a game runs. Verify this works before opening anything.
  3. Open the shell. Remove the battery, undo the tri-wing case screws, and separate the halves carefully. The two boards are joined by a delicate ribbon cable, so lift gently and note how everything sits before disconnecting.
  4. Install the screen upgrade. For an IPS kit, disconnect the original LCD ribbon, seat the new FunnyPlaying Q5 panel, and route its flex cable as the instructions show. On a DMG, test-fit and trim a little plastic from the screen frame so the glass sits flush. For a backlight kit, fit the light layer behind the existing LCD instead.
  5. Add contrast and audio mods. If you are bivertion, rotate the polarizer and solder the hex inverter now while the board is exposed. Tap the prosound point, wire in the amp, and swap in the new speaker. Keep solder joints clean and check for bridges.
  6. Reassemble and test. Reconnect the ribbon, tuck the wiring so nothing is pinched, and screw the halves back together loosely at first. Power on and check the image, all buttons, the speaker, and headphone output. Only fully tighten once everything works.

If something is wrong, reopen and inspect before forcing the case shut; pinched ribbons and cold solder joints are the usual culprits and are easy to fix while the shell is still loose.
