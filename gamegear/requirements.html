What you'll need

What You'll Need

A Game Gear restoration is mostly a soldering job, so the bulk of your shopping list is tools rather than parts. Gather everything before you open the shell — stopping halfway to order a missing tip is how boards sit on benches for months.

  • A complete capacitor kit — the most important item on this list. Buy a curated full-recap kit from Console5, ZedLabz, or Retrosix matched to your board revision. Do not cherry-pick a few caps; replace them all.
  • A temperature-controlled soldering iron with a fine conical or chisel tip. Surface-mount work rewards precise heat control.
  • Leaded solder (63/37 or 60/40) for easy flow, plus a small amount of fresh flux. Beginners find leaded solder far more forgiving.
  • Flux paste or pen — essential for clean SMD joints and for cleaning up corroded pads.
  • Solder wick (desoldering braid) and optionally a solder sucker for removing old joints.
  • Isopropyl alcohol (90%+) and cotton swabs to scrub away leaked electrolyte and flux residue.
  • A digital multimeter to check continuity and confirm corroded traces still conduct after cleanup.
  • Fine tweezers for placing the new surface-mount capacitors and observing polarity.
  • A tri-wing screwdriver (Sega used tri-wing security screws) plus a small Phillips driver.
  • A plastic spudger or guitar pick to separate the shell halves without scratching.
  • Magnification — a headset loupe or USB microscope makes inspecting pads and polarity dramatically easier.
  • Optional upgrade parts — a McWill LCD kit, an IPS LCD kit, an EverDrive-GG flashcart, or AV/controller mod boards, depending on your plans. These come after the recap.

If you have never soldered surface-mount components, practice on a scrap board first. The Game Gear is a satisfying project, but it is not the place to learn your very first joint.
