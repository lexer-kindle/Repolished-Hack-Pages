The Three Main Upgrades, Compared

SNES modding is not a single procedure but a set of independent upgrades you can mix and match. The three that matter most are the FXPak Pro flashcart, a switchless region mod, and an RGB video upgrade. Each solves a different problem, and most enthusiasts end up doing all three.

FXPak Pro flashcart

The FXPak Pro is the most impactful single purchase. It is an FPGA cartridge: rather than emulating, it reconfigures programmable logic to behave like the original mapper hardware. That gives it near-full library coverage, including games that depended on special chips such as the SuperFX, SA-1, DSP, and CX4. It also supports MSU-1, the audio system that lets fan hacks stream CD-quality music and even FMV.

Crucially for importers, the FXPak Pro does its own automatic region patching and comes in a multi-region shell, so it boots NTSC and PAL ROMs without touching the console. It requires zero soldering. The trade-off is cost and the fact that it does nothing for original cartridges or for video quality.

SuperCIC / Super CIC++ region mod

A switchless region mod replaces or supplements the console's lockout chip so that imported original cartridges boot, and so you can switch between 50Hz and 60Hz on demand. SuperCIC and the newer Super CIC++ are the community-standard solutions. Both are "switchless," meaning you change region by holding the Reset button or entering a controller button combination instead of drilling a physical toggle into the shell. An LED is often wired to show the current mode by colour.

This is the right path if you collect real cartridges from multiple regions. It does require soldering several fine wires to the cartridge slot and CIC area. If you only ever play from the FXPak Pro, you can skip it, since the flashcart already handles region.

RGB bypass amplifier

The video upgrade depends on your board. 1CHIP consoles already output clean RGB, so an amp offers only a marginal gain. Older multi-chip boards produce a softer image and clearly benefit from a THS7374-based RGB bypass amp, which buffers the raw signal for a sharper, better-defined picture. The SNES Jr (SNS-101) is the special case: it has no usable RGB on the multi-out from the factory and requires an amp to produce proper RGB at all.

In short: buy the FXPak Pro for the library, add SuperCIC++ if you run real imports, and fit an RGB amp if your board is multi-chip or an SNES Jr. Pair the result with a quality RGB cable to see the difference.
