Super Nintendo Hacks Guide

Modding the Super Nintendo

The Super Nintendo Entertainment System has no internal firmware to update or hack, so "modding" here means something different than on later consoles. There is no software exploit to chase. Instead, every meaningful upgrade comes from hardware: an SD-based flashcart for running your library off a card, a region mod so imported cartridges and PAL/NTSC games boot, and video work to coax a sharp RGB picture out of the machine.

The flashcart of choice is the FXPak Pro (formerly sold as the SD2SNES). It is an FPGA cartridge that re-creates the SNES mapper chips in programmable logic, so it runs an enormous slice of the library, including titles that relied on special enhancement chips, plus MSU-1 streamed-audio hacks. It also performs automatic region patching and ships in a multi-region shell, which sidesteps many import headaches on its own.

For cartridges run on the bare console rather than the flashcart, region freedom comes from a switchless mod such as SuperCIC or Super CIC++. These let you change region and toggle 50/60Hz by holding Reset or entering a controller combo, with no ugly toggle switch drilled into the shell.

The picture is the other big project. Whether your console outputs clean RGB depends heavily on its board. 1CHIP consoles give a noticeably crisper native image, while older multi-chip boards look softer and benefit from an RGB bypass amplifier. The compact SNES Jr (SNS-101) actually requires an amp to produce proper RGB at all.

Before buying anything, identify two things: whether your console is a 1CHIP unit or an older multi-chip board, and which region it is. Those two facts drive every decision that follows in this guide.
